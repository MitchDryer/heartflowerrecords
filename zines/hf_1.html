The word ‘realm’ describes a kind of semi physical, semi emotional space.

Our day to day experience is really not one of passing through physical spaces but one of passing through realms which include the constant and always fluctuating emotional state of the body. Neither the emotional state nor the physical state ever exist independently; you never perceive a space without an emotional filter and an emotion is never felt without a physical context. The more clearly this is seen the more the seeming duality of an internal and external world begins to collapse and life becomes a fluid singular experience. Because of course it is only through interpretation that we imagine ourselves to be at some deep hidden place at the centre of our experience interacting with an outside world separate from ourselves. If we allow ourselves to drop this interpretation even for a moment we can find ourselves in a place that no longer feels definable as physical or non physical space but maybe more suited to the label of realm.

Freedom is maybe something like an expansion of possibility or a collapsing of inevitability. What once was defined solid and real melts into a fluid field of potential and you realise yourself as the creative desire itself orchestrating the unfolding. The pace of imagination more closely matches the pace of doing cognition more closely matches intuition and the abstract is seen to be as real as the real is seen to be abstract. These qualities can be found in many areas of life but especially in something like a creative practice in relationship with others or anything that induces some sort of flow state. The resistances we face in bringing creations into this world are in essence the same resistances we face when we attempt to create a better world for ourselves, and by overcoming them in our creative practice we move closer to overcoming them in our day to day life. Maybe ultimately an ongoing experience of freedom is possible and by tasting it in creative areas of our life we are learning how to bring it into totality.

A realm of freedom is then freedom experienced not in a physical place or an emotional state or someplace else but as the totality of experience. In a moment of true inspiration or creative breakthrough not only does the creative project experience freedom the ground you stand on is seen to be freedom.

The computer in front of you is seen to be freedom the pains of the body are seen to be freedom and even the world outside is seen to be freedom.

Maybe it is a mistake to view our lives as a compromise between work and leisure or obligation and freedom because when we enter into a realm of freedom everything is liberated.

the narrative/conceptual element of this album is really cool. what is your interpretation of the orb?

the orb is a physical embodiment of the freedom and liberation that the world so desperately needs. it holds the potential to transform the world and help it to access its truest state, but in the album, the orb itself is imprisoned in a tesseract of walls and unable to do this transformative work, so it becomes the work of the collective to set the orb free, and in doing so allow everything and everyone else to become free.

this album covers a lot of sonic ground. what musical influences do you think made their way into BTO?

daphnis et chloé by maurice ravel. there is something so magical and otherworldly about its choral sound and its peaks and swells of intensity. i tried to bring a similar intensity and vibe to the choir writing and overall sound of the album. i listened to this recording and pictured the orb in the early stages of this project when i was at an emotional low, and it felt so important to persist and transcend all doubts - to put all the necessary life force into making the album honour the orb itself.

it’s time by max roach is such a bonkers album. it influenced BEHOLD THE ORB, especially in the way the choir and jazz quartet are integrated together. the intro to sunday afternoon, the third track on that album also inspired the intro to break it free.

i love the way the album purest form by james francies uses ethereal reverby vocal and synth sounds to create a dark and magical world which brings the freaky modern jazz improvisations into a new realm. especially the unison vocals in the track 713. the titles also really resonate with the vibe of BEHOLD THE ORB - levitate, transfiguration, purest form all evoke the kind of energy which the orb does.

i’m always inspired by work coming out of the LA creative jazz scene. henry solomon and logan kane have lots of great solo records and a project together called outside world. their albums are always fearless curations which draw from a wide sonic palette, but also all sound so much like them. i like artists who have their own vibe which transcends genre, or who can make each musical element sound like them. maximilian sink mastered their album outside world 3 and i asked him to master BEHOLD THE ORB as well. he did an amazing job making my crazy sonically varied album sound unified.

the song orbital rim was definitely inspired by the dunedin sound, music from where i grew up. especially asta rangu’s EP plasticine and my friends’ band re:ruby who have a single out and play around wellington a lot. i would listen to plasticine a lot to try and get orbital rim closer to that sort of mix. my dunedin friends approve of that track so i feel it was a success.

when you write music, how do you balance taking inspiration from other artists with finding inspiration in yourself?

i have a pretty specific taste but i’m learning to appreciate music which does things in a way i wouldn’t. i’m inspired by the elements of other people’s work that i find strangest, most exciting, novel, beautiful, authentic etc. i try and find a home for the ones which resonate with me. i’ve heard people subscribe to the notion that there is nothing new under the sun but i’m interested in conjuring up new sounds and trying to disprove this theorem!

the tracks with Frida are magical. How was it writing and recording those?

i wrote a lot of these in the weeks before the quartet recording day, fueled by nervous energy and excitement. i layered choir patches together until i was happy with the sound, and moved between writing on my midi keyboard and drawing crazy chords with my computer mouse. i don’t think about harmonic rules when i write music, i just imagine what i want to hear and draw from all the music i’ve listened to and loved as well as embrace and curate the surprises which emerge in the process.

when i showed the songs to frida flacks she instantly saw the vision, and elevated it to heights beyond what i was imagining. frida captured the emotional, transcendent, pained and ethereal in all the right moments and harmonised a lot of my melodies, intuitively adding voices layer by layer in fresh and unexpected ways. we recorded her vocals over the course of 3 sessions, DIY style on my laptop and talked about the orb, cosmic energy and transcendence over tea breaks. i feel frida lifted the album to the next level. once the vocals were added i felt ready to finish the album, and spent a few weeks working obsessively on it until it was done.

the mix of a live jazz band with the ethereal sounds you gravitate toward in your production gives the album a feeling of having one foot grounded in reality and one foot floating in space. why do you think there’s value in music that extends into the abstract?

i’m glad that feeling comes across. it is a really important part of the storytelling on the album - the worldly and otherworldly coexisting or colliding is what happens in the story! i think it also expresses the dissonance i often feel between the cosmic truths/love and the constraints and cruelty of the material world and its oppressive/bureaucratic systems and such.

BEHOLD THE ORB is largely about dreams and grand visions of what the world can be without appealing to existing authority or hegemony, and this grandeur and connection to the abstract and cosmic aims to counter defenses that authority and bastions of the status quo use. too often ‘common sense’ and ‘logic’ are weaponised and argued for as the epitome of truth while dismissing the intuitive and the power of creativity, compassion and dreams. a lot of people think that we can’t change the world because their rules and visions are based only upon what is already dominant, not what is possible.

the title of this issue is “realms of freedom.” do you find that creating music gives you access to a realm of freedom?

definitely. i feel that i am able to create new realities rather than just participate in existing ones, which is especially important when the dominant realities aren’t rooted in important things like freedom or equality. it’s about creating places i can exist within where i can be as weird, playful and true as i want to be. hopefully my realms end up being important to others too, and can extend beyond symbolism, allowing people to return to truth beyond the walls, to relinquish their internal compliance to rules which aren’t rooted in truth.
